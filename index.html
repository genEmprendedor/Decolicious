Location

At The Core of Edmonton

Nowhere is the intersection of life and work so remarkable and vibrant as it is in downtown Edmonton. The neighbourhood of McCauley is growing in residential, retail and office space. Buildings are landmarks of old and new ventures, a melting pot for retail and commerce. Edmonton's best-loved business, art galleries, museums, luxury hotels, and fusion cuisine are all claiming a stake in redefining Edmonton's downtown.
All readily available for tenants of Decolicious.

The neighbourhood is famous for its collection of religious assemblies, representing a variety of faith groups. In particular, 96 Street (in McCauley) is also known as "Church Street," and was once cited by Ripley's Believe it or Not as having the largest concentration of churches in the world.

McCauley is currently host to 8 official City of Edmonton sites designated as Municipal Historic Resources, wich Decolicious is one of them. These resources are legally protected by bylaws from demolition and inappropriate alterations − these resources are also eligible to receive municipal assistance. McCauley is only exceeded by Downtown in terms of number of sites on the registry.

An Iconic Neighbourhood

McCauley is a vibrant and ethnically diverse inner city neighbourhood in Edmonton; out of 272 neighbourhoods, McCauley is the 11th most walkable. Chinatown and Little Italy are both present in McCauley. Both are unique and distinct retail shopping areas representing over 250 shops and services.

The beautiful Red Gates signify the entrance to Edmonton’s Chinatown, a vibrant shopping and dining destination brimming with Asian culture and flair. In addition to the Chinese population in this area, there is a growing Vietnamese population has added to the cultural diversity. Historic buildings dot the area, while new and upgraded structures are popping up as part of the renewal of the area.

In Little Italy, Giovanni Caboto Park, as it is known today, was built as the Gyro Park in 1913. In 1980 the park was renamed in honour of the Italian community and the concentration of restaurants and shops featuring Italian foods and merchandise. Renovated and reopened on July 9, 2006, the park now features a new entrance, a replica of an Italian fountain and an interactive life-sized statue of Frank Spinelli by local artist Zazo.
